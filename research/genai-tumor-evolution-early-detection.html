Large Language Models in Single Cell Cancer Genomics

Developing transformer-based foundation models to understand and generate cancer progression patterns, with applications to clinical trial data analysis and early detection through electronic health records

Research Overview

Our laboratory pioneers the application of Large Language Models (LLMs) to single-cell cancer genomics, developing novel AI methodologies to decode tumor evolution and enable clinical translation. We have created evoCancerGPT, the first transformer model for zero-shot single-cell cancer progression prediction, and are leading efforts to apply foundation models to real-world clinical trial data from the Hale Center for Pancreatic Cancer Research.

Clinical Impact

Our LLM-based approaches are transforming cancer research by enabling: (1) mechanistic understanding of tumor evolution at unprecedented resolution, (2) biomarker discovery from clinical trial genomic data, and (3) early detection capabilities through analysis of 1M+ patient electronic health records from the Mass General-Brigham-Dana Farber medical system.

Key Projects

evoCancerGPT - Zero-shot Cancer Progression Prediction

We developed evoCancerGPT, a decoder-only transformer architecture specifically designed for single-cell genomics data. The model treats cancer evolution as a generative process, learning to predict future cellular states from current tumor snapshots. This enables zero-shot prediction of cancer progression patterns without requiring paired temporal data.

Architecture Innovation

Custom transformer with 4096 token context, specialized MLP layers for sparse expression matrices

Training Data

100,000+ single cells ordered by pseudotime from multiple cancer types

Zero-shot Capability

Generalizes to unseen cancer types without additional training

Key Achievements

  • First transformer model for single-cell cancer evolution prediction
  • 90%+ accuracy in predicting cell state transitions
  • Identifies novel evolutionary trajectories validated experimentally
  • Manuscript in preparation for Nature Methods (2025)

Foundation Models for Clinical Trial Analysis

We evaluate and adapt state-of-the-art foundation models, including scGPT, to analyze single nucleus RNA sequencing data from ongoing clinical trials at the Hale Center. Our work focuses on the Paricalcitol trial, examining pre- and post-treatment samples from metastatic pancreatic cancer patients.

Clinical Trial Integration

  • Analysis of 50+ patient samples from Paricalcitol trial (NCT03883919)
  • Identified biomarkers predicting treatment response
  • Developed interpretable cell type annotation using foundation models
  • Presented at AACR Pancreatic Cancer Conference 2024

Reasoning LLMs for Bioinformatics Tasks

We implement general reasoning LLMs, including DeepSeek-R1, as alternatives to traditional statistical models for cell type annotation and biological interpretation. This approach leverages reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) to improve accuracy and interpretability in genomic analyses.

Technical Innovation

  • First application of reasoning LLMs to single-cell annotation tasks
  • Achieved expert-level performance on benchmark datasets
  • Provides interpretable reasoning chains for biological insights
  • Reduces annotation time from hours to minutes

LLMs for Pancreatic Cancer Early Detection

Leading development of LLM methodology to analyze electronic health records from 1M+ patients in the Mass General-Brigham-Dana Farber system. This initiative aims to identify high-risk patients 1-3 years before clinical diagnosis, potentially improving survival rates from 12% to 50-70%.

Data Scale

1M+ patient records with longitudinal clinical data

Multi-modal Integration

Clinical notes, lab results, imaging reports, medications

Clinical Validation

Prospective trial planned for 2026-2027

Future Directions

We are expanding our LLM capabilities to include: (1) multi-modal foundation models integrating spatial transcriptomics and imaging data, (2) mechanistic interpretability methods for biological insight generation, (3) reinforcement learning enhancements for improved reasoning, and (4) multi-institutional collaborations for model validation. Our vision is to establish LLMs as fundamental tools for cancer research and clinical care.
