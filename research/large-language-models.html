Large Language Models in Cancer Genomics

Pioneering the application of generative AI and transformer architectures to understand and generate cancer progression patterns, revolutionizing how we analyze single-cell genomics data and predict clinical responses in oncology.

Research Overview

Our lab is at the forefront of developing and applying Large Language Models (LLMs) specifically designed for cancer genomics. We leverage the power of transformer architectures and generative AI to tackle some of the most challenging problems in computational oncology. Our approach treats genomic data as a language, where genes are words, cells are sentences, and cancer progression becomes a narrative that we can both understand and predict.

Revolutionary Impact

This research represents a paradigm shift in cancer genomics, enabling zero-shot prediction of cancer evolution, mechanistic interpretability of tumor progression, and the generation of synthetic cancer progression data for therapeutic development. Our models are the first to successfully apply natural language processing techniques to single-cell cancer genomics at scale.

Project A: Multi-modal LLMs for Genomics

Foundation Models for Multi-omics Integration

We are developing the next generation of foundation models that can simultaneously process and integrate multiple types of genomic data - transcriptomics, proteomics, and epigenomics - within a unified transformer architecture. This represents a significant advancement over current single-modality approaches.
Technical Approach
  • Multi-modal transformer architecture with cross-attention mechanisms
  • Custom tokenization strategies for different omics data types
  • Self-supervised pre-training on large-scale cancer genomics datasets
  • Fine-tuning for downstream tasks including drug response prediction
PyTorch Transformers Hugging Face Single-cell RNA-seq Proteomics Multi-modal Learning
Key Innovation

Our multi-modal architecture can learn shared representations across different biological data types, enabling the model to make predictions even when some data modalities are missing - a common challenge in clinical settings.

Project B: evoCancerGPT - Zero-shot Cancer Evolution

Generating Cancer Progression with Generative AI

evoCancerGPT is our flagship large language model specifically designed to understand and generate cancer evolutionary trajectories. Using a decoder-only transformer architecture, the model can predict the next steps in cancer progression given initial cellular states.

evoCancerGPT Architecture Diagram

Decoder-only transformer with MLP layers for sparse expression matrices

Figure: Overview of evoCancerGPT architecture showing data preprocessing pipeline, sentence construction from 4096 tokens representing cells in pseudotime order, and transformer architecture with extra MLP layers to handle sparse scRNA-seq data and non-linear relationships.
Model Architecture
  • Decoder-only transformer with 4096 token context length
  • Evolutionary sentences constructed in increasing pseudotime order
  • Extra MLP layers before and after transformer blocks
  • Specialized handling of sparse expression matrices
  • Zero-shot generation capabilities for unseen cancer types
Training Data & Performance

The model is trained on comprehensive single-cell RNA sequencing datasets from multiple cancer types, enabling it to learn universal patterns of cancer evolution. In validation studies, evoCancerGPT successfully generated realistic cancer progression trajectories that matched known biological patterns and predicted novel evolutionary pathways validated by experimental follow-up.

Project C: LLM Applications in Clinical Trial Data

Foundation Models for Real-World Cancer Clinical Data

We evaluate and apply state-of-the-art foundation models like scGPT to real-world single nucleus RNA sequencing data from ongoing clinical trials at the Hale Center for Pancreatic Cancer. This work bridges the gap between research models and clinical applications.
Clinical Trial Integration
  • Analysis of Paricalcitol clinical trial data (Vitamin D receptor agonist)
  • Pre-treatment and post-treatment single nucleus RNA sequencing
  • Foundation model fine-tuning on clinical genomics data
  • Biomarker discovery for treatment response prediction
  • Integration with clinical outcome data

Clinical Trial Workflow

Paricalcitol trial with pre/post treatment analysis

Overview of the Paricalcitol clinical trial conducted at the Hale Center, evaluating the effect of Paricalcitol (vitamin D receptor agonist) combined with gemcitabine and albumin-bound paclitaxel in metastatic pancreatic cancer patients.

Clinical Impact

This work directly impacts patient care by providing clinicians with AI-powered tools to predict treatment responses and identify patients most likely to benefit from specific therapies. Our models have shown superior performance compared to traditional biomarkers in predicting clinical outcomes.

Project D: Reinforcement Learning Enhanced LLMs

RLHF for Single Cell Genomics Tasks

We implement reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) techniques to improve LLM performance on single cell genomics tasks, particularly cell type annotation. This approach, inspired by our collaboration with DeepSeek-R1, enhances model reasoning capabilities.
Reinforcement Learning Framework
  • Human feedback integration for cell type annotation quality
  • Reward model training based on expert biological knowledge
  • Policy optimization for improved reasoning chains
  • Multi-step reasoning for complex cell state identification
  • Continuous learning from expert corrections
Key Achievements

Our RLHF-enhanced models demonstrate significant improvements in cell type annotation accuracy, particularly for rare cell types and transitional states. The models also provide interpretable reasoning chains that help biologists understand the decision-making process.
