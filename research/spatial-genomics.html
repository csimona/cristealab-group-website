Spatial Genomics & AI

Revolutionizing pancreatic cancer research through spatial modeling and analyses using clinical trial patient genomic and imaging data, integrating cutting-edge spatial transcriptomics with computational pathology.

Research Overview

Our spatial genomics research integrates high-resolution spatial transcriptomics, proteomics, and computational pathology to understand the complex tumor microenvironment in pancreatic cancer. We develop AI-powered analysis frameworks that can map cellular interactions, identify resistance mechanisms, and predict treatment responses at unprecedented spatial resolution.

Clinical Translation

This work directly impacts patient care by providing spatial biomarkers for treatment response prediction, identifying resistance mechanisms in KRAS inhibitor therapy, and developing personalized treatment strategies based on tumor microenvironment characteristics from ongoing clinical trials at Dana-Farber.

Project A: Spatial Transcriptomics Analysis Frameworks

CosMx Spatial Analysis Pipeline Development

We are developing comprehensive computational frameworks for analyzing high-resolution spatial transcriptomics data from the Nanostring CosMx platform. Our pipeline integrates spatial gene expression analysis with computational pathology to provide multi-scale insights into tumor architecture.
Pipeline Components
  • Quality control and normalization for CosMx spatial data
  • Cell segmentation and molecular assignment algorithms
  • Spatial neighborhood analysis and cellular interaction mapping
  • Integration with H&E histopathology images
  • Multi-resolution spatial analysis from subcellular to tissue-level
  • Statistical frameworks for spatial hypothesis testing

Data Processing

Advanced algorithms for handling the unique challenges of spatial transcriptomics data, including spatial autocorrelation, technical artifacts, and multi-scale analysis.

Visualization Tools

Interactive visualization platforms for exploring spatial gene expression patterns, cellular neighborhoods, and tissue architecture.

Statistical Methods

Novel statistical approaches for spatial differential expression analysis and identification of spatially variable genes.

CosMx Platform Spatial Transcriptomics Image Analysis Python/R Pipelines Statistical Modeling

Project B: Fibroblast Spatial Diversity in Pancreatic Cancer

Mapping Fibroblast Heterogeneity in Primary and Metastatic Disease

We investigate the spatial organization and functional diversity of cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) in primary pancreatic tumors and metastatic sites. This work reveals how fibroblast subtypes contribute to tumor progression and treatment resistance through spatial analysis of clinical samples.
Research Objectives
  • Characterize fibroblast subtypes using spatial transcriptomics
  • Map fibroblast-cancer cell interactions in tumor microenvironment
  • Compare primary vs. metastatic fibroblast spatial organization
  • Identify fibroblast signatures associated with treatment resistance
  • Develop spatial biomarkers for patient stratification

Clinical Relevance

Cancer-associated fibroblasts are key drivers of pancreatic cancer progression and treatment resistance. Our spatial analysis reveals how different fibroblast subtypes create therapeutic barriers and identifies opportunities for combination therapies targeting the tumor microenvironment.

Key Findings

Our analysis has identified distinct spatial patterns of fibroblast organization that correlate with patient outcomes. Specific fibroblast subtypes form protective niches around cancer cells, contributing to drug resistance and immune evasion. These findings are informing new therapeutic strategies.

Project C: Spatial Analysis of Collagen-Stroma Associations

Extracellular Matrix Architecture in Pancreatic Cancer

We develop novel computational methods to analyze the spatial relationship between collagen deposition, stromal cell organization, and cancer cell invasion patterns. This work combines imaging mass cytometry with spatial transcriptomics to understand how ECM remodeling drives tumor progression.
Analytical Approaches
  • Multi-modal integration of spatial transcriptomics and proteomics
  • Collagen fiber orientation and density analysis
  • Spatial correlation analysis between ECM and cellular components
  • Machine learning models for ECM pattern classification
  • Mechanistic modeling of tumor-stroma interactions
Clinical Applications

The dense stromal environment of pancreatic cancer creates barriers to drug delivery and promotes resistance. Our spatial analysis identifies ECM patterns that predict treatment response and reveals opportunities for stroma-targeted therapies to improve drug penetration and efficacy.

Extracellular Matrix Collagen Analysis Stromal Biology Multi-modal Integration Drug Delivery

Project D: Spatial Features of KRAS Inhibition Resistance

Understanding Resistance Mechanisms Through Spatial Analysis

In collaboration with Revolution Medicines, we analyze spatial features of resistance to KRAS inhibition in pancreatic cancer using data from the RMC-6236 clinical trial. This work identifies spatial biomarkers of resistance and maps how resistant cell populations emerge and spread within tumors.

RMC-6236 Clinical Trial

We are analyzing spatial transcriptomics and proteomics data from patients treated with RMC-6236, a next-generation KRAS inhibitor. This represents one of the first comprehensive spatial analyses of KRAS inhibitor resistance in clinical samples, providing unprecedented insights into resistance mechanisms.

Resistance Analysis Framework
  • Pre-treatment and post-treatment spatial profiling
  • Identification of resistant cell populations and their spatial distribution
  • Analysis of cell-cell communication networks in resistance
  • Spatial mapping of compensatory signaling pathways
  • Development of predictive models for resistance emergence
Therapeutic Implications

Our spatial analysis reveals that resistance to KRAS inhibition often emerges in specific microenvironmental niches. Understanding the spatial patterns of resistance will inform combination therapy strategies and help predict which patients are most likely to benefit from KRAS-targeted treatments.

Project E: High-plex Protein Profiling in PDAC

Spatial Proteomics Analysis Framework

We develop comprehensive frameworks for analyzing high-plex spatial proteomics data in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC). This work integrates imaging mass cytometry with spatial transcriptomics to provide multi-dimensional views of the tumor microenvironment at protein and RNA levels.
Proteomics Pipeline
  • High-dimensional protein expression analysis in spatial context
  • Cell phenotyping and functional state identification
  • Protein-protein interaction network analysis
  • Integration with spatial transcriptomics data
  • Identification of spatial protein biomarkers
Multi-omics Integration

By combining spatial proteomics with transcriptomics, we can identify post-translational modifications, protein localization patterns, and functional protein complexes that are not detectable through transcriptomics alone. This provides a more complete picture of cellular function in the tumor microenvironment.

Spatial Proteomics Imaging Mass Cytometry Multi-omics Integration Protein Networks High-plex Analysis

Project F: Foundation Models in Computational Pathology

Counterfactual Perturbations of Histopathology Images

We implement cutting-edge foundation models for computational pathology to generate counterfactual perturbations of histopathology images. This work allows us to simulate treatment effects and understand how therapeutic interventions change tissue architecture.
Foundation Model Applications
  • Pre-trained histopathology foundation models (e.g., PathLDM, HistoGen)
  • Counterfactual image generation for treatment simulation
  • Integration with spatial transcriptomics data
  • Morphological feature extraction and analysis
  • Automated pathology assessment and scoring

Revolutionary Approach

This work represents a new paradigm in computational pathology where we can generate synthetic images showing how tissues might respond to treatments. This capability will accelerate drug development and help predict treatment responses before therapy initiation.
