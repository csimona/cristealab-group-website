Breast Cancer Genomics

Single-cell analysis of tumor initiation, immune escape mechanisms, and clinical response prediction with applications to prevention and precision therapy

Research Overview

Our breast cancer research program focuses on understanding the earliest events in cancer development, from stem cell transformations to immune escape during the transition from pre-invasive to invasive disease. We employ single-cell genomics, spatial analysis, and clinical trial integration to identify novel prevention strategies and predict treatment responses, with the ultimate goal of preventing cancer before it becomes clinically apparent.

Paradigm Shift

Our research represents a fundamental shift from treating established cancers to understanding and preventing their formation. By identifying protective stem cell populations and developing mRNA vaccine targets, we aim to transform breast cancer prevention, particularly for high-risk individuals with BRCA mutations or strong family histories.

Tumor Initiation & Stem Cell Biology

Novel Stem Cell Population for Breast Cancer Prevention

We discovered a novel stem cell population responsible for breast cancer prevention in rat models. This breakthrough finding challenges conventional understanding of cancer initiation and opens new avenues for prevention-based therapies, including mRNA vaccine development.

Key Discovery

  • TGFβ inhibition triggers long-term depletion of cancer progenitor cells
  • Age-dependent decrease in tumor incidence post-treatment
  • Effective prevention of postmenopausal ER+ breast cancer
  • Potential for short-term preventive interventions
Experimental Model

NMU carcinogen-exposed rat models with TGFβ pathway inhibition

Analysis Methods

scRNA-seq, FACS purification, connectome analysis

Clinical Relevance

Pregnancy-induced protection mechanisms

Secretory Basal Cells as Prevention-Associated Progenitors

We identified and characterized secretory basal cells (SBCs) as progenitors associated with breast tumor prevention. These cells serve as potential therapeutic targets for developing mRNA vaccines and other prevention strategies.

Research Highlights

  • SBCs identified across multiple mammalian species including humans
  • Main source of insulin-IGF signaling in mammary epithelium
  • Gene signatures predict reduced survival in ER+ breast cancer
  • Deep learning models for cell annotation and characterization

mRNA Vaccine Development

Our characterization of SBCs provides specific molecular targets for developing preventive mRNA vaccines that could enhance protective mechanisms in high-risk individuals.

Immune Escape & TCR Repertoire Analysis

Peripheral Blood TCR Diversity as Biomarker

We analyzed peripheral blood T-cell receptor (TCR) clonotype diversity in 485 breast cancer patients, revealing age-associated patterns that predict cancer progression and recurrence. This work enables non-invasive monitoring through liquid biopsy approaches.

Clinical Findings

  • Lower TCR diversity in older patients regardless of stage
  • TCR-α diversity predicts de novo stage IV disease in younger patients
  • Higher diversity associated with recurrence in older DCIS patients
  • Distinct blood transcriptome profiles based on TCR diversity

Cycling T Regulatory Cells Drive Immune Escape

We discovered a novel cycling T regulatory cell population (cycTreg) that orchestrates the immunosuppressive environment during the transition from ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) to invasive breast cancer, providing new targets for immunotherapy.

Status: Under review at Nature Immunology (co-senior author)

Major Discoveries

  • cycTregs identified as primary orchestrators of immune escape
  • OX40 agonist + anti-PD-L1 enhances CD8+ T cell infiltration
  • CAF-Treg cross-talk critical for cycTreg expansion
  • cycTregs predict recurrence in grade 1 DCIS
Patient Samples

DCIS and invasive breast cancer with spatial topology

Model Systems

Rat tumors recapitulating human breast cancer

Therapeutic Testing

OX40 agonist and checkpoint inhibitor combinations

Clinical Trial Integration & ADC Response

Clinical Trial Biopsy Collection Framework

We established a systematic framework for collecting fresh tissues during diagnostic breast biopsies, enabling real-time single-cell analysis in clinical trials. This infrastructure supports translational research while maintaining clinical workflow efficiency.

Implementation Success

  • 68.4% consent rate (270/395 patients)
  • 446 research specimens collected safely
  • High diagnostic concordance with clinical biopsies
  • Successful single-cell transcriptomic analyses

Koh SB et al. "Systematic tissue collection during clinical breast biopsy is feasible, safe and enables high-content translational analyses" npj Precision Oncology 5:85, 2021

Predicting ADC Response in Triple-Negative Breast Cancer

We conducted spatio-temporal profiling of Sacituzumab Govitecan response in the NeoSTAR trial, analyzing 50 patients with localized TNBC. Our integrated analysis reveals predictors of pathologic complete response beyond TROP2 expression levels.

Response Predictors

  • Chronic interferon signatures associated with residual disease
  • CXCL13+ exhausted CD8+ T cells enriched in complete responders
  • Suppressive M2 macrophage interactions predict resistance
  • Quiescent tumor cells in immune-poor neighborhoods resist therapy
Trial Design

Pre/post treatment biopsies from NeoSTAR trial

Analysis Methods

scRNA-seq, 16-plex Orion imaging, exome sequencing

Clinical Impact

Biomarkers for patient selection and combination strategies

Future Directions

While our breast cancer research continues with established collaborators at Dana-Farber and Harvard, we are translating our methodological innovations to pancreatic cancer research. The single-cell analysis frameworks, spatial profiling techniques, and immune modeling approaches developed for breast cancer provide a strong foundation for advancing pancreatic cancer early detection and treatment strategies at the Hale Center.
