Breast Cancer Genomics

Advancing breast cancer research through spatial and temporal single cell analyses of tumor initiation, malignant transformations, and clinical response prediction for innovative therapeutic strategies.

Research Overview

Our breast cancer research program focuses on understanding the earliest events in breast cancer development, from initial cellular transformations to immune evasion mechanisms. We employ cutting-edge single-cell genomics and spatial analysis techniques to map the evolution of breast cancer at unprecedented resolution, with the ultimate goal of developing prevention strategies and improving treatment outcomes.

Paradigm Shift: From Treatment to Prevention

Our research represents a fundamental shift from treating established breast cancers to understanding and preventing their formation. By studying the earliest cellular events in breast cancer development, we aim to identify intervention points that could prevent cancer before it becomes clinically apparent, potentially through mRNA vaccines and other preventive strategies.

Tumor Initiation & Stem Cell Transformations

Project A: Novel Stem Cell Population for Breast Cancer Prevention

We have discovered a novel stem cell population responsible for breast cancer prevention in rat models. This groundbreaking finding challenges conventional understanding of breast cancer initiation and opens new avenues for prevention-based therapies, including the potential development of mRNA vaccines.

Revolutionary Discovery

This research identifies specific cellular populations that actively prevent tumor formation, rather than simply lacking tumorigenic potential. Understanding these protective mechanisms could lead to the development of preventive therapies that enhance natural tumor suppression mechanisms in high-risk individuals.

Research Methodology
  • Single-cell RNA sequencing of mammary stem cell populations
  • Lineage tracing of protective stem cell subsets
  • Functional validation through transplantation assays
  • Comparative analysis across cancer-resistant and susceptible models
  • Identification of prevention-associated molecular signatures

Clinical Translation Potential

This discovery has immediate implications for breast cancer prevention strategies. We are working to translate these findings into clinical applications, including the development of mRNA vaccines that could enhance protective stem cell function in high-risk women, such as those with BRCA mutations or strong family histories of breast cancer.

Project B: Secretory Basal Cells as Prevention-Associated Progenitors

We have identified secretory basal cells as progenitors associated with breast tumor prevention. This research reveals how specific cellular differentiation programs can protect against malignant transformation and identifies new targets for prevention-based interventions.
Secretory Basal Cell Characterization
  • Single-cell transcriptomics of mammary epithelial populations
  • Spatial mapping of secretory basal cell distribution
  • Functional analysis of secreted factors and paracrine signaling
  • Developmental trajectory analysis from progenitors to differentiated cells
  • Comparison between normal and pre-malignant tissue environments

Protective Secretome

Secretory basal cells produce a unique set of factors that create a protective microenvironment against tumor initiation. Understanding this secretome could lead to the development of preventive therapies that mimic or enhance these natural protective mechanisms.

Spatial & Temporal Immune Analyses

Project A: Peripheral Blood TCR Clonotype Diversity

We investigate peripheral blood T-cell receptor (TCR) clonotype diversity as an age-associated marker of breast cancer progression. This work reveals how immune repertoire changes can serve as early biomarkers for cancer development and progression.
TCR Repertoire Analysis
  • High-throughput TCR sequencing from peripheral blood samples
  • Longitudinal tracking of clonotype dynamics
  • Age-stratified analysis of immune repertoire diversity
  • Correlation with cancer progression and clinical outcomes
  • Integration with tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte analysis

Published Impact: PNAS 2023

This work was published as a co-first and co-senior author paper in PNAS (Jun Nishida*, Simona Cristea*+, et al., 2023). The study demonstrates that TCR diversity patterns in blood can predict breast cancer progression, providing a non-invasive biomarker for disease monitoring and potentially early detection.

Liquid Biopsy Applications

This research enables the development of blood-based tests for breast cancer monitoring and early detection. TCR repertoire analysis could be incorporated into routine screening programs, particularly for high-risk populations, providing a minimally invasive method for cancer surveillance.

Project B: Cycling T Regulatory Cells in Immune Escape

We have identified a subpopulation of cycling T regulatory cells that creates an immune escape hub during the transition from in situ to invasive breast carcinoma. This discovery reveals a critical window for immune intervention and provides new targets for immunotherapy.

Critical Transition Point

The transition from ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) to invasive breast cancer represents a critical juncture where early intervention could prevent cancer progression. Our identification of cycling T regulatory cells as key mediators of this transition provides specific targets for preventing cancer invasion.

Immune Escape Mechanism Analysis
  • Single-cell analysis of immune populations in DCIS vs. invasive cancer
  • Spatial mapping of T regulatory cell distribution
  • Cell cycle analysis of regulatory T cell populations
  • Functional validation of immune suppression mechanisms
  • Identification of therapeutic targets for immune restoration

Under Review: Nature Immunology

This groundbreaking work is currently under revision at Nature Immunology as a senior author publication. The findings provide new insights into immune evasion mechanisms and identify specific time points and cellular targets for immune-based interventions to prevent cancer progression.

Therapeutic Implications

Understanding how cycling T regulatory cells facilitate immune escape opens new avenues for early intervention. Targeting these cells during the DCIS stage could prevent progression to invasive cancer, fundamentally changing breast cancer treatment from reactive to preventive.

Triple-Negative Breast Cancer Clinical Applications

Project A: Single Cell Analysis Framework for Clinical Trials

We have developed comprehensive frameworks for biopsy collection and single-cell analysis in clinical trial settings for breast cancer patients. This infrastructure enables real-time analysis of treatment responses and biomarker discovery in clinical contexts.
Clinical Trial Integration
  • Standardized biopsy collection protocols for single-cell analysis
  • Real-time processing of clinical samples
  • Integration with clinical trial databases and outcomes
  • Quality control measures for clinical-grade genomics data
  • Regulatory compliance for translational research

Published Framework: npj Precision Oncology 2021

Our systematic approach to tissue collection during clinical breast biopsy was published in npj Precision Oncology (Siang-Boon Koh, Brian N Dontchos, et al., including Simona Cristea, 2021). This work demonstrates that comprehensive translational analyses are feasible and safe in routine clinical practice.

Project B: Spatio-temporal Profiling of Antibody-Drug Conjugate Response

We conduct comprehensive spatio-temporal profiling of antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) response in triple-negative breast cancer clinical trial data, focusing on Sacituzumab Govitecan treatment. This work aims to predict treatment responses and optimize ADC therapy strategies.

Sacituzumab Govitecan Analysis

Sacituzumab Govitecan represents a breakthrough in triple-negative breast cancer treatment, but not all patients respond to this therapy. Our spatial and temporal analysis of tumor samples from treated patients aims to identify predictive biomarkers and understand resistance mechanisms to optimize patient selection and treatment strategies.

Multi-dimensional Analysis
  • Pre-treatment and post-treatment single-cell profiling
  • Spatial analysis of ADC distribution and response
  • Temporal tracking of cellular responses to treatment
  • Integration of genomic, transcriptomic, and proteomic data
  • Predictive modeling for treatment response
Clinical Impact

This research directly impacts patient care by identifying biomarkers that can predict response to Sacituzumab Govitecan before treatment initiation. These predictive models enable personalized treatment selection and help identify patients who would benefit from alternative therapies.

Manuscript in Preparation

High-resolution profiling of antibody-drug conjugate response in triple-negative breast cancer (Liu et al., manuscript in preparation, to be submitted in 2025). This work represents one of the most comprehensive analyses of ADC response mechanisms in clinical samples.
